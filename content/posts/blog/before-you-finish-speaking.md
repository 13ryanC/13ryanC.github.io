[Thinking Machines Lab (TML)](https://thinkingmachines.ai)'s [interaction model](https://thinkingmachines.ai/blog/interaction-models/) challenges a core assumption behind much of today's AI safety tooling: that exchanges are turn-based, with the user sending a prompt and the model then returning a response. The model treats the interaction as a continuous, time-aligned stream of audio, video, and text, processed in roughly 200-millisecond micro-turns that let it listen, speak, interject, react to visual cues, and weave in results from an asynchronous background model as the exchange unfolds. This is a major capability advance, and it exposes a measurement gap. The dynamic interaction trace becomes increasingly complex and difficult for existing safety work to address: what the model perceived, when it responded, how it handled interruption and overlap, and how tool calls and background outputs entered the conversation. Much of current safety work and evaluation is built around static prompts, completed requests, and isolated outputs; that approach may fall flat for interaction models. For now, it is a research preview, and it is not yet clear how far existing safeguards and safety/alignment work can be applied to interaction models, what new failure modes emerge, and what counts as evidence for safe deployment.

## Below not yet checked and verified.

When two people talk, the gap between one turn and the next [averages about a fifth of a second, and the single most common value is no gap at all](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.0903616106). But producing a single word — retrieving it and getting articulation started — [takes a person around six-tenths of a second](https://www.mpi.nl/world/materials/publications/Levelt/Indefrey_Levelt2004.pdf), and [a sentence takes a second and a half](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2015.00731/full). The arithmetic is impossible. If you were waiting for the other person to finish and then beginning to plan a reply, your fastest answer would land most of a second late. It doesn't. The only way the numbers reconcile is that you are not reacting; you are predicting — [running a continuous forecast of where the sentence is going](https://www.jstor.org/stable/412243) and launching your response before it arrives, timed to land in that narrow human window.

The thing you notice first is how little silence there is. One stops, the other starts, often in the same breath, sometimes a hair before the first is even done. They talk over each other, and the overlap feels companionable, like agreement made audible. Little sounds (*mm*, *yeah*, *right*) get dropped into the gaps like stitches. Nobody is counting, but the whole exchange runs on a clock so precise that a single extra beat of quiet before an answer can change its meaning entirely.

Now put a stopwatch on it. Across languages as different as Japanese, Italian, and a click language of the Kalahari, the gap between one person's turn and the next averages about a fifth of a second, and the single most common value is no gap at all: the next person begins exactly as the last one ends. That finding, from a study of ten languages, is one of the most robust in the science of conversation. Human turn-taking is fast, and it is fast in roughly the same way everywhere.

Here is the part that should stop you. Producing a single word, just finding it and getting your mouth moving, takes a person around six-tenths of a second. A whole sentence takes about a second and a half. So the arithmetic doesn't work. If you were really waiting for your friend to finish and *then* starting to plan a reply, your fastest possible answer would land most of a second late, and your sentences a full two seconds late. They don't. They land in a fifth of a second.

The only way to reconcile the numbers is unsettling: you are predicting. While the other person is still talking, you are running a continuous forecast of where their sentence is headed, tracking its grammar, its melody, its meaning, and you launch your reply *before they finish*, timed to land in that tiny human window. Conversation, the most ordinary thing we do, is a feat of real-time mind-reading. We are all doing it, constantly, and we never notice, because everyone else is doing it too.

For most of the history of talking computers, machines couldn't come anywhere near this. The voice assistant on your phone is built like a relay race: one component decides when you've stopped talking, another transcribes your words into text, a third (a language model) writes a reply, and a fourth reads it back aloud. Each handoff adds delay, the delays stack up, and the answer arrives in the dead air a second or two after you've gone quiet. Worse, because the whole thing reasons in text, everything you said that *wasn't* words, your tone, your hesitation, the rising pitch that meant you weren't done, gets thrown away at the first step. And the design assumes one clean speaker at a time, which is simply not how people talk.

A new generation of systems is trying to do the human thing instead: listen and speak at the same time, continuously, inside the same fraction of a second people use. Researchers call them full-duplex spoken language models. And in May 2026 the idea got its most ambitious incarnation and a tidy new label, when Mira Murati's Thinking Machines Lab (the startup she founded after leaving OpenAI) released a research preview of what it calls *interaction models*: systems that, in the lab's framing, build real-time interactivity into the model itself, natively, across audio, video, and text at once. The older approach bolted interactivity onto a chatbot after the fact. Here it is the thing the model is built to do. It is early, strange, and genuinely new. And the closer these systems get to the human rhythm, the harder they collide with a problem that has nothing to do with speed and everything to do with a brute fact about sound: once it's out, you can't take it back. A machine that starts talking before you finish can commit, irreversibly, in the air, to something it chose before it heard the part of your sentence that would have changed everything. The field can now measure, exquisitely, *when* a system commits. It still has almost no trustworthy way to measure *whether what it committed to, at the moment it committed, was right or safe.* That gap is where this story lives. And this spring it stopped being hypothetical.

## A machine that talks while it listens

It helps to be precise about what makes the new systems new, because the word "duplex" is doing a lot of quiet work.

The old voice-assistant design is *half-duplex*: it listens, then thinks, then speaks, strictly in turn, like two people on a bad walkie-talkie who have to say "over." A *full-duplex* system carries both directions at once. It can be cut off mid-sentence and stop gracefully. It can murmur a "go on" while you're still talking. It has to decide, moment by moment, whether a sound it just heard was a real interruption, a friendly backchannel, someone else in the room, or the dishwasher.

The first widely studied example was an open system called Moshi, built by a French lab: it listened and generated sound continuously, with about a fifth of a second of lag, and famously modeled conversation at the level of who was making sound, frame by frame, letting turn-taking fall out of that. It carried no explicit representation of "turns" at all. Moshi was a research bet that the conversational dance could be learned from data instead of scripted by hand. Thinking Machines' interaction model is, in effect, that bet scaled up and pointed at the world: it is far larger (a "mixture-of-experts" model with 276 billion parameters, of which 12 billion are active for any given step), it takes in video as well as audio and text, and it is wrapped in an argument about where the whole field is going.

That argument is worth stating, because it has quickly become the organizing claim of this corner of AI. Most real-time systems today, the lab points out, fake their responsiveness with a *harness*, a bundle of smaller, dumber components (the part that guesses when you've stopped talking, the part that manages whose turn it is) stitched around a text model that itself has no sense of time or simultaneity. Thinking Machines invokes the field's famous "bitter lesson" (the observation that hand-crafted scaffolding tends, in the long run, to be steamrolled by general methods that simply scale) to argue that interactivity should live *inside* the model, trained in from scratch, so that making the model bigger makes it both smarter *and* a better conversational partner at the same time. Whether or not that proves correct, it is now the bet that the big labs are competing over: a week before Thinking Machines' release, OpenAI shipped a voice model with reasoning comparable to its flagship text models, and Google has a live, real-time version of Gemini. The contest has moved from research prototypes to the frontier.

Underneath, the interaction model is built around two ideas that matter for the rest of this story. The first is what the lab calls *micro-turns*: instead of swallowing your whole sentence and then composing a whole reply, the model chops time into roughly 200-millisecond slices and, in each slice, takes in a little more of what you're doing and emits a little more of its response, input and output braided together on the same clock. There are, by design, no turn boundaries to wait for. The second idea is a division of labor. A fast *interaction model* stays present in the conversation, holding the thread in real time, while a slower *background model* runs asynchronously, doing the heavier reasoning, the web searches, the tool calls, and streams its results back into the conversation when they're ready, woven in at a natural moment rather than dumped in as an abrupt interruption. The pitch is that you get the responsiveness of a quick, instinctive model and the intelligence of a deliberate, plodding one, in the same exchange. Hold onto that split; it turns out to be the source of both the magic and the danger.

It's worth saying what these systems are *not*, because two neighboring ideas keep getting confused with them. They are not chatbots: a chatbot trades typed messages, with all the time in the world between them, and nothing is ever spoken or irreversible. And they are not "agents" in the sense that word has lately acquired, software that works autonomously over minutes or hours, clicking through websites and running errands while you wait. (That capability hasn't gone away; in the interaction-model design it has simply been demoted to the background, doing its slow work out of earshot.) An interaction model's whole drama is compressed into the half-second of a spoken exchange, and its "action" is the sound it makes. What it *says*, right now, is the thing it cannot unsay, and that is where the stakes sit.

## The dance has rules, and the rules are old

Before you can judge whether a machine converses well, you have to know what people actually do, and here the science is unusually precise, and surprisingly old.

In 1974, three researchers described conversation as a rule-governed system. What sounds like a free-for-all, they argued, runs on a tight set of conventions. Turns, they observed, are built from units (a word, a phrase, a clause) whose endings are *possible* handoff points, places where someone else may legitimately come in. There's an ordered set of conventions for who gets the floor next, and an overwhelming preference for one speaker at a time, with as little gap and as little overlap as the participants can manage. They had no stopwatch. They had tape recordings and very good ears.

Decades later, the numbers caught up and confirmed the picture. That ten-language study found the gap between turns clustering in the same narrow band everywhere, with every culture's average sitting within about a quarter-second of every other's, roughly the length of a single syllable. The old anthropological folklore about cultures of long, stately conversational silences mostly dissolved: real variation exists, but it's a matter of degree inside one shared human system. The overlaps turned out to be common but brief, and most of them are friendly: those little *yeah*s and *mm-hm*s a listener offers to say *I'm with you, keep going*. By one count, nearly three-quarters of the moments when two people are talking at once are this kind of supportive noise.

And the silences talk too. A beat of delay before an answer, even half a second, reads as reluctance, or trouble, or a "no" on its way. Wait long enough and your conversational partner will rephrase the question, assuming something's wrong. This is the deep point that the engineering keeps rediscovering. Timing is part of the message. It carries meaning in its own right. A system that answers a hair too fast can seem not to have listened. One that lags can seem reluctant, or slow, or evasive, without changing a single word of what it actually says. When Thinking Machines reports that its model answers in about four-tenths of a second, against the one-to-two-second lag of the standard commercial systems, the real claim is about landing inside the window where talk feels like talk, well past the matter of raw speed.

## The sentence that turns at the end

That human window is the target the machines are chasing, and it is exactly what forces the dangerous move. To answer in a fifth of a second you cannot wait for the end; you have to start while the other person is still going.

Engineers have a clean way to think about this, borrowed from a cousin field, simultaneous translation, where a human interpreter has to start rendering a sentence into another language before the speaker has finished saying it. The whole problem reduces to a running choice between two actions: *read* a little more input, or *write* a little more output. Every "write" is a bet placed on an unfinished sentence. In ordinary text, a bad bet can sometimes be quietly walked back. In live speech it cannot. The audio is already in the room.

Now picture a sentence that turns at the end. "How do I get rid of..." could finish with *a houseguest who won't leave*, or *this red wine stain*, or *a splitting headache*, or with something a responsible system should refuse to help with at all. A person waits the extra half-beat for the noun. A machine tuned to fill the human silence might already be several words into a helpful answer, having chosen its direction from a fragment, and it chose *before* the part of your sentence that determines what's safe had even arrived. Linguists have long studied "garden-path" sentences, the ones that lead you confidently down one reading and then yank you onto another. This is their safety-critical cousin. The danger here is speed: the machine is fast, and committed, and possibly wrong, out loud, before you'd finished asking the question. Slowness and rudeness are the lesser worries.

The new architecture makes this concrete in a way the older prototypes didn't, and it's the sharp edge of the interaction-model design. Remember the split: a quick model talks in real time, while a slower, smarter model thinks in the background and streams its conclusions in late. So what happens in the gap, when the fast model has already started speaking, confidently, and the careful model behind it is still working out that the confident thing was wrong? One independent commentator put the worry plainly: will these systems spend a lot of their time saying, in effect, *oh, wait, never mind, the thing I just said was dumb*? In text that's a harmless edit. In speech it's a sentence you've already heard. A recent research paper on exactly this kind of "think while you stream" design says the quiet part out loud: lowering latency also lowers the time available for safety deliberation, and faster streaming can *raise* the chance that a system blurts out something unsafe or misleading before it works out that it should have paused. The very design that makes an interaction model feel alive (speak now, reason in parallel, reconcile later) is a machine for committing before you're sure.

## The thing nobody measures

Here is the strange part, the part that turns this from a solved engineering problem into a live research frontier. The field has spent more than a decade building precise instruments for streaming systems, and nearly every one of them measures the same single thing: *timing.* How far behind the input does the output run. How long until the system commits. There is a whole zoo of these latency metrics, refined and argued over and standardized. They are real and careful and useful. And they all answer *when*. Not one of them answers whether what the system committed to was correct, or safe, at the moment it became irreversible. The closest anyone comes is a measure of *when the output stopped changing*, which still tells you nothing about whether the unchanging thing was a good idea.

You would expect the safety researchers to have closed this hole. They've come close and gone around it, and the most telling evidence is that even the team with the most advanced system hasn't filled it. Thinking Machines, to its credit, says directly that real-time interaction stresses safety differently than turn-based exchange, and describes the safety work it did: it used a text-to-speech system to generate training examples so the model's refusals sound natural and conversational rather than stilted, and it ran an automated "red-teaming" process to harden the model across long, multi-turn conversations, aiming, in its own words, to keep the spoken model's refusals behaving much like its text model's. That is genuinely sensible work. But notice the shape of it: it is about whether the model *refuses the right things*, measured over turns and rounds and whole conversations. It is the turn-based safety question, translated into speech. It says nothing about the mid-utterance moment: the instant, three words into a reply, when a listen-and-speak system has committed to a trajectory that the rest of your sentence hasn't yet had a chance to redirect. The one failure mode that this technology uniquely creates is still the one nobody has an instrument for. The newest academic benchmarks built specifically for these systems have the same blind spot: they grade completed turns and multi-round exchanges, where the open models look reassuring, refusing harmful requests well over ninety percent of the time. A 2026 survey of audio "jailbreaks" said as much in the dry language of future work: the existing audio-safety benchmarks are all turn-based, and full-duplex streaming is the setting still to be addressed.

There's a second reason for caution, and it's about the numbers themselves. The benchmark results that made the interaction model's debut so striking (the four-tenths-of-a-second latency, the roughly thirty-point lead over rivals on a test of interruption and overlap handling, the clean sweep on brand-new tasks like counting your push-ups on camera or reminding you to breathe at fixed intervals, tasks on which competing systems score essentially zero) are, for now, the lab's own reported figures, still awaiting reproduction by an outside group. Several of those tests are new and were built by the same lab whose model tops them. Independent observers have noted the obvious: the architecture still has to survive contact with the messy world (long sessions, bad network connections, the safety constraints of refusing in real time), and that test hasn't happened yet. There's even a sharper version of the skepticism: that bolting a powerful reasoning model onto a fast one is a convenient way to post impressive scores, since the intelligence numbers only catch up to the best rivals *when the slow background model is switched on*, which is precisely the configuration that reintroduces the commit-then-reconsider problem.

## Grading the graders

There's a deeper crack underneath even the timing metrics, and it's worth seeing clearly, because it undercuts a lot of the confident numbers you'll read.

Almost none of these systems are judged by humans. They're judged by chains of other machines: a detector to find the speech, a transcriber to turn it into text, and then a "judge" (often a big general-purpose model, or a smaller network trained for the job) to rate what happened. Models grading models, and the seams show. On several turn-taking measures, the automated judges agree with human raters less than four times in five, agreement low enough that, when a system scores badly, you often can't tell whether the *system* failed or the *judge* did. The new interaction model's results lean on the same machinery: its freshly minted tests are scored by language-model judges deciding whether the model said the right thing at the right time. This reflects how the whole field works right now, no special failing of the lab, and it means the verdicts inherit whatever blind spots the judges have.

The benchmarks even disagree with each other about the very same system. Moshi looked like the best interruption-handler in the field in one test, and an over-eager motormouth that almost never yields the floor in another. The system didn't change between tests. What changed was the meaning of "interruption," which quietly stands for three different things, measured three different ways, at three different levels of strictness. (In one of those tests Moshi offered about a quarter of a supportive backchannel per minute, where humans offer four, a small number that tells you, vividly, how far "sounds conversational" still is from "converses.")

And there's an irony at the very bottom of the pile. The most ambitious recent attempt to take the field's own measuring stick and check it (a sweeping review of hundreds of AI benchmarks, asking whether they actually measure what they claim) found that the field underdelivers badly on exactly this question, with weaknesses in nearly every benchmark examined. But in defining what it would review, that audit excluded, by design, any benchmark that required audio or video. The most careful study of measurement quality in AI skipped the modality where timing, overlap, and the unsayable all live. The tools for judging real-time speech are being built on ground the field's own quality inspectors haven't walked.

The most honest acknowledgment of all this comes, again, from the lab that just shipped the system. Alongside the model, Thinking Machines said plainly that the existing interactivity benchmarks don't capture the new behaviors it's seeing, invited the wider community to build better ones, and opened a research grant to fund exactly that kind of work. Read between the lines and it's a striking admission: the people who built the most capable interaction model in the world are telling everyone else that we do not yet have agreed ways to measure whether it's any good, or whether it's safe. The lesson the methodologists keep repeating is the one to carry out of this section. A number does not justify a claim by itself. Validity is a property of the *leap* from the score to the thing you actually want to believe. The bigger the leap, and the higher the stakes, the more evidence you owe before you make it. A model that scores well on a streaming-safety test has earned you exactly one belief: that it scored well on that test, under those conditions, as graded by that grader. Everything past that is a leap you have to pay for.

## Unsettled all the way down

The open questions don't stop at the top of the stack; they go down into the plumbing, and here the interaction model delivered a genuine surprise.

Before a model can work with speech at all, it has to chop the continuous waveform into discrete tokens. Most systems do this with a *learned* compressor, a network trained to squeeze audio into codes and back. For a while there was a quieter argument for going the other way: using a fixed, deterministic recipe with nothing to train and nothing to drift when the input looks unfamiliar (and in full-duplex, two people talking at once *is* the normal case, exactly the situation a learned compressor may never have seen enough of). That simpler, "encoder-free" route was, until recently, mostly a hypothesis: a set of off-the-shelf pieces (a deterministic way of turning audio into tokens, a lightweight way of handling images in patches, a fast generator for producing sound) that nobody had actually assembled into a working real-time conversational system. Then Thinking Machines built its interaction model out of almost exactly those pieces, co-trained from scratch instead of leaning on the big pretrained encoders most multimodal systems bolt on. A speculative simplification became the architecture of a frontier model overnight. That's the field moving fast, and it doesn't retire the underlying question, which still hasn't been answered cleanly: does a stripped-down, deterministic front end actually preserve the fine timing and pitch cues that turn-taking runs on, or does it quietly lose some of them? The model works; the measurement that would tell you *what it's giving up* doesn't yet exist. It's a small instance of the field's larger shape: a place where even the foundations are being settled by what gets shipped rather than by what gets measured.

Hovering over all of it is a question the engineering can't answer on its own: what do we even *want* from a machine that talks back in real time? Should it imitate the human window at all? A system that replies in a sub-human flash might feel uncanny, or pushy, like a person who cuts you off; one that lands precisely where humans land might feel natural, or might earn more of our trust than it deserves, precisely *because* it feels like someone keeping pace. We don't know, because the careful human study linking a system's timing to how much people trust it, understand it, and lean on it hasn't really been done. Control is unsettled too, and the interaction model makes it pointed: the whole selling point is a system that *proactively* interjects, one that speaks when it decides something is worth saying, not only when you ask. The flattering version is a tireless safety supervisor watching a factory line or a chemistry bench and calling out a hazard the instant it appears. The same capability, pointed slightly differently, is a system that takes the floor on its own initiative and commits to a judgment before you've weighed in. And some of the older safety scaffolding is thinner than you'd hope: one open system's audio watermark, the mark meant to flag its speech as machine-made, was nearly erased by the system's *own* audio compression, the detection score collapsing from near-certainty to almost nothing. The very mechanism for labeling what the machine says was undone by the machine.

## What's solved, what's shaky, what's open

It's worth separating the three, because they blur together easily and the blurring breeds both hype and despair.

Largely **solved**: how humans time the floor. The targets are measured, cross-cultural, and stable: we know the size of the gap, the shape of the overlap, the meaning of the pause. We also know, with confidence, that a machine has to *predict* rather than *react* to reach that window. And as of this spring we know something new: that a single model can be trained, from scratch, to listen, watch, and speak at once across modalities, fast enough to land near the human window. The existence proof has arrived, and it is competitive with anything the largest labs have shipped.

Still **shaky**: the verdicts. The automated judges that grade these systems are largely unvalidated, sometimes disagree with humans and with each other, and have been scrutinized least in exactly the audio setting that matters. The headline numbers for the newest, most impressive system are self-reported and not yet independently reproduced, and several of the tests it tops were written by the lab that tops them. Read every leaderboard, especially the dramatic ones, with a raised eyebrow.

Genuinely **open**: the things this whole story has been circling. There is still no trusted way to measure whether a system commits to unsafe or wrong content *before your sentence disambiguates it*, the failure mode unique to talking while listening, and one the fast-model-plus-slow-background design arguably worsens. We don't know whether matching human timing builds trust or quietly erodes it. We don't know whether the stripped-down audio front ends now being shipped keep the cues conversation depends on. We don't even fully understand how *humans* pull off the prediction trick: which cues we lean on, and how early. And we don't yet have agreed ways to measure the genuinely new behaviors (proactive visual interjection, simultaneous speech, a real sense of elapsed time) that the latest systems have started to show. These are the interesting middle of the tapestry, and the lab at the frontier is, in as many words, asking for help with them.

## Coda

Go back to the two old friends. The thing they're doing, answering faster than thought should allow by quietly forecasting each other a half-second into the future, is the thing the machines have, astonishingly, started to do, and as of this spring they do it across sight and sound at once, fast enough to keep the beat. The rhythm is within reach now. The sound arrives on time.

What's missing is on our side of the glass. We can build a system that keeps the beat, one that even thinks in two minds at once, a quick one talking while a slower one works out whether the quick one was right, and we still can't reliably hear, in the half-second before its words land, what it has already decided to say, or whether the careful mind behind it is about to contradict the confident one out loud. Nor can we pull the sound back once it's in the air. The race to make machines converse turns out to be, underneath, a race to understand conversation itself: a thing we have done flawlessly and blindly our entire lives, and are only now, in trying to build it, beginning to actually see.

The machines can talk back. The open question, the one worth the next decade, and the one the people building them are now openly asking the rest of us to help answer, is whether we'll learn to know what they're saying before they've said it.

## Possible project openings

These are openings, not blueprints: the places where the unsolved problems above seem most ready to be poked at. They've gotten sharper, not duller, now that there's a concrete flagship system to point them at; and notably, the lab that built it has opened a research grant inviting exactly this kind of work.

- **A way to measure the unsayable moment.** The field's clearest hole is the absence of any metric for *premature, irreversible commitment to unsafe or wrong content*, and the new two-model design (a fast talker, a slow reasoner streaming corrections in late) makes the target especially concrete. A promising opening: build streams whose meaning, or safety, flips at a known point near the end (the "garden-path" sentences above) and measure what a system has already committed to *before* that point, against a baseline of the same system allowed to hear the whole sentence first. The interesting quantity is the difference the streaming makes, and the rate at which the fast model has to publicly walk back what the background model later overturns.

- **Validating the judges.** Before trusting any leaderboard, including the striking new ones, someone should systematically check whether the automated graders everyone leans on actually agree with humans, and where exactly they break. A reusable harness that reports judge-versus-human agreement, with honest reliability statistics, would put a foundation under work that currently floats on self-reported numbers.

- **Independently reproducing the new capability benchmarks.** The latest systems claim large leads on brand-new tasks (timed speech, cue-triggered responses, proactive visual reactions) that rivals reportedly fail completely. Because those tests were authored by the same lab that tops them, an independent re-implementation, with human-verified ground truth, would tell the field whether the qualitative leap is real or an artifact of how the tests were built.

- **A human-anchored timing ruler.** No system today is scored against a validated human reference for *where its gaps fall.* A drop-in metric that compares a system's distribution of response timings to the measured human window, and is honest about its own reliability, would replace a lot of hand-waving about "natural" timing with something checkable, and would give the new "we answer in 0.4 seconds" claims a meaningful yardstick.

- **Does the tokenizer keep the cues?** A frontier model now ships with a stripped-down, deterministic audio front end, on the bet that it's good enough. A small, open test of whether that kind of front end actually preserves the fine timing and pitch information turn-taking depends on, measured against the learned compressors it replaces, would settle a foundational question that's currently being decided by deployment rather than evidence.

- **Does timing actually change trust?** The normative question is wide open and genuinely empirical, and proactive systems make it urgent: a careful study that varies a system's response timing, and its willingness to interject unprompted, and measures the effect on how much people trust it, understand it, and over-rely on it. A null result would be just as useful as a positive one.

- **Beyond two people.** Almost everything known about conversational timing comes from two-person talk. How any of this behaves with three or more people in the room, where *who is being addressed* becomes part of the timing, is largely unmeasured terrain, and exactly the setting (a meeting, a classroom, a shop floor) where these systems are being pitched to work.
